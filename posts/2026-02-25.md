## üì£ RUN FOR THESE ROLES
  
During our last meeting, we talked about encouraging people to run for Precinct Committee Officer (PCO) and State Delegate roles as one of the highest bang for your buck ways to get involved in Democratic politics in terms of time committment and impact.

---
## üèòÔ∏è Precinct Committee Officer (PCO) Roles**
  
A **PCO represents Democrats in your local precinct**, which is the smallest voting unit.

PCOs typically:
- Vote on **local party leadership**
- Help shape **party rules and priorities**
- Assist with organizing, canvassing, and turnout
- Serve as a bridge between voters and the party
  
**Why it's worth applying::**
- Turnout for PCO races is extremely low
- Many seats go completely uncontested
- You can win with a handful of votes or be appointed if a seat is vacant

In most states, PCOs are elected during the primary with no one running opposed or appointed by the local party if no one runs
---

## **üèõÔ∏è State Delegates**

This role might be more sexy as they have a larger area of influence. State delegates attend **state party conventions or caucuses** and at those conventions delegates vote on:

- State party leadership
- Party rules and bylaws
- Party platform language
- Sometimes endorsements
- In presidential years, they chose the national delegates for the national convention.
    
---

## **üá∫üá∏  State Delegates vs National Delegates**

This might be a point of confusion (it was for me) but state delegates are not the same as the national delegates that get sent to the DNC when we nominate presidential candidates:

- **State delegates**
    - Work inside the state party
    - Attend state conventions
    - Help shape rules, leadership, and platforms for their state
    - Often help select nationoal delegates for the Democratic National Convention in presidential years
        
- **National delegates**
    - Attend the Democratic National Convention
    - Usually vote to confirm the winner of their state‚Äôs presidential primary
    - Are mostly bound to vote for whoever wins their state primary/caucuse (although technically they can chose not to)
  
National delegates are **usually a rubber stamp** for the primary winner but when there is no majority, that's when the fabled "contested convention" happens which is every political journalists's wet dream. The last time it happened was in 1968 which was chaotic and because of that state parties have instituted rules so that it doesn't happen again.

But state delegates can still have a big influence because:

- They help decide _who_ becomes a national delegates
- Who in turn determine national party platforms
- Which in turn shape the internal culture and direction of the party

---

## üóÇÔ∏è Conventions and Caucuses  

While rules vary by state, the process is usally pretty simple:
1. People run for **PCO** or attend local caucuses
2. PCOs and caucus attendees elect **state delegates**
3. State delegates attend the **state convention**
4. State conventions:
    - Elect leadership
    - Set rules and platform
    - Influence national delegate selection
    - State delegates and PCOs are often easy to win
	    - They run UNOPPOSED most times
	    - If you attend the convenction/caucuse and put a btd in to run, you have a big chance of winning

---

## **Why this holds a lot of "bang for your buck"**

Running for PCO or becoming a state delegate:
- Requires relatively little time
- Has outsized influence as you'll be giving input to your state pary
- Gives you voting power inside the party
- Builds organizing credibility if you choose to network
